3D printer with parallel kinematics

I performed the present project together with two colleagues as part of our engineering thesis. We divided the joint project into three parts - electronic, software and mechanical, for which I was responsible. Each of us wrote an engineering paper on the corresponding part.

As I mentioned earlier, the topic of my engineering thesis was to design the structure and mechanical solutions of the printer. The key aspect that guided me during the design process was the openness of the design. During my review of the market, I noticed that there was a shortage of deltas that would easily would make it possible to interfere with the mechanical structure and thus modify the printer. When it comes to printers Cartesian printers, there are already many more such printers (an example is the Creality Ender 3 printer, which I I own and modify myself). The result of my work is a prototype printer with parallel kinematics, in which without major problems you can replace the extruder, motors or even the head. The project uses many printed components, also the head mount is printed. The natural reaction to this information is to doubt whether it is sure to print the head mount, which heats up to temperatures above 200 degrees Celsius, is a good idea, considering the fact, that 3D printed objects are not very heat-resistant. It turns out that... yes. The use of appropriate cooling (in our project it is a 40x40 fan), as well as designing the mount in a way that allows the free flow of air allows the printer to operate without problems. He uses a similar solution in his printer, which has been working this way for more than a year and a half. In our delta, too, this solution works perfectly, which we were able to confirmed through the prototype. Thanks to the fact that the element is printed and in addition from PLA, it is possible to replace it relatively simple replacement. The consumer only needs to design the mount for the head he wants to use, print it and mount it. There is no need to buy any mounts or have the component milled.

Models of the LCD display, Arduino Mega, limiters, Traxxas mounts, GT2 gear, bearings, fans, and any connecting components were downloaded from open source sites or from the manufacturer's catalogs.

Printing area

The main change from the previous version is the working field and the size of the printer in general. It was planned to be a 210x260 mm cylinder, but after assembling the prototype, due to Traxxas connections, it turned out, that the effector has problems reaching the edge of the working field in the parallel plane. For this reason, the safety, the dimensions of the working field were changed to 180x290 mm.

Printing time comparison

Above is a screenshot from Cura. It shows the predicted printing times printing for a Cartesian printer (right) and for a printer with parallel kinematics (left). As you can can be seen, the printing times on the delta are significantly shorter. In fact, I also observed a difference in printing time in favor of the delta.

Heat bed of the printer

At the very top of the table is a glass top, which is designed to provide a maximum flat printing surface. Glass, because of its rigidity, is well suited for this purpose. Lower down is an aluminum heating plate, which heats the glass, which in turn improves adhesion of the prints. At the very bottom is a chipboard, which acts as insulation. The whole thing rests on horizontal profiles and is fixed with special parts bolted together.

Effector - exploded view

Shown above is a exploded view of the effector. Its design provides excellent rigidity. The head placed in this holder is superbly restrained, with no backlash. The attached 5015 turbine provides excellent cooling of the print.
